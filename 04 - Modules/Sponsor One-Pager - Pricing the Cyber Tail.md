# RIM Case Study — Pricing the Cyber Tail
### How Orbital Dynamics Technologies turned "we're afraid of a cyber attack" into a board decision

> One-page sponsor/exec brief. All figures are drawn from the ODT canonical dataset and are **order-of-magnitude by design**; ODT is an illustrative composite, not a real company.

---

## The situation
Orbital Dynamics Technologies (ODT) operates a 56-satellite connectivity constellation. FY2028 is its **first profitable year — revenue $270M, EBIT +$13M** — and the board is building toward a **~$250M Nasdaq IPO in H1 2029**. Free cash flow is **−$40M**, negative by design while Phase-4 capacity is built. One number governs everything: a **−$100M FCF liquidity trigger**. Cross it, and ODT must take a distressed, dilutive raise *before* the IPO — and the story the company is selling to public markets evaporates.

## The challenge
The board knew it was exposed to a cyber attack on its ground segment. What it could not do was **price that fear** — and you cannot govern, insure, or capitalise a risk you cannot put a number on. "Spend more on security" and "buy more insurance" were the only available answers, and neither told the board what it actually bought.

## The approach — RIM + SPICE
ODT ran the threat through **RIM**, modelling the risk not as a checklist item but as a node in a graph of business consequences. The hero scenario — **S1, a NOC / ground-segment compromise** — was elicited as a structured business event and quantified across three cases (best / realistic / pessimistic), in the board's own currency: **EBIT and free cash flow.**

The result was a distribution, not an adjective:

| S1 — NOC compromise | EBIT (3-yr) | FY2028 FCF | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Best (P10) | −$40M | −$60M | absorbed within plan |
| **Realistic (P50)** | **−$69M** | **−$79M** | turns +$13M EBIT into ≈ −$41M |
| Pessimistic (P90) | −$110M | **−$118M** | **breaches the −$100M trigger** |

That last row is the whole point: in the pessimistic case a *cyber* event stops being an IT problem and **becomes a financing event** — it crosses the liquidity trigger, forces the distressed raise, and lands on the IPO. In the model this is a literal edge: **S1 → FN1 (IPO Window Collapse)**. And S1 is only one of **six risk families** — supply-chain, regulatory, geopolitical, financial and programme risks all converge on the same place: the IPO. RIM exists to make that convergence visible and therefore *defendable*.

## The decision — return on spend
With the tail priced, the board could finally compare **money spent** against **loss removed**:

- The comfortable answer — the existing **$50M cyber-insurance tower** — *does not pay* for this event. Its **war / state-actor exclusion** is written against exactly S1's attacker profile, and attribution takes days. Paper coverage, not real coverage.
- The two controls that actually move the curve — **separating the shared Denver/Dublin identity plane (~$4M)** and **a clean control-plane recovery capability (~$1.5M)** — cost **~$5.5M, one-time.** SPICE shows them collapsing the pessimistic case **back above the −$100M trigger.**

| | Figure |
|---|---|
| Control spend (one-time) | **~$5.5M** |
| Realistic feared loss removed | **~$69M** |
| Return on spend | **≈ 12×** |
| What it protects | the **~$250M IPO** (FY2028 FCF held above the trigger) |

## The outcome
For the price of a rounding error on the P&L, ODT **severed the path from a cyber event to a collapsed IPO** — and replaced an unpriceable fear with a *modelled, bounded, disclosable* residual it could put in front of investors. The board didn't buy "security." It bought **a quantified movement in the distribution**, on the same slide as the loss it avoided.

> **The shift RIM delivers:** from "are we secure?" (unanswerable) to "what do we spend, and what does it buy?" (a board decision). Same method, pointed next at the other five families that converge on the IPO.
