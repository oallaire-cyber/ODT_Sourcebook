# Exec Briefing — The Risks That Converge on the IPO
### Using RIM to govern, price, and capital-allocate against a single objective: a successful H1 2029 listing

> Board / sponsor briefing (≈ 2 pages). Companion to the one-pager [[Sponsor One-Pager - Pricing the Cyber Tail]]. All figures are from the ODT canonical dataset and are **order-of-magnitude by design**; ODT is an illustrative composite, not a real company.

---

## 1. The one objective everything points at
ODT enters FY2028 as a **$270M-revenue, +$13M-EBIT** operator — its first profitable year — heading for a **~$250M Nasdaq IPO in H1 2029** (objective **TCO-04**). Free cash flow is **−$40M** by design while Phase-4 capacity is built, and the board governs to one bright line: a **−$100M FCF liquidity trigger**. Below it, ODT must take a distressed, dilutive raise *before* it can list — and the equity story collapses.

Every material risk the company carries is ultimately a risk *to that listing.* The board's job is not to eliminate risk; it is to **keep FY2028 FCF above the trigger** and put a credible, disclosed risk picture in the IPO file.

## 2. The insight RIM delivers: the risks are a graph, not a list
A conventional risk register shows independent line items. RIM shows what they actually are — **a causal network that converges on one node.** Six risk families, with utterly different causes, all land on the same financial sink, **FN1 (IPO Window Collapse)**, which is the scenario of *failing TCO-04*:

| Family | Feared event | ≈ Realistic cost | Reaches the IPO via |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cyber (S1)** — *hero* | NOC / ground-segment compromise | **$69M** | S1 → FN1 (Strong) |
| **Supply chain (SC1)** | Supplier build-chain intrusion | $36M | SC1 → FN1 (Strong) |
| **Regulatory (RG1)** | Accreditation suspension | $48M | RG1 → FN1 (Strong) |
| **Geopolitical (GP1)** | Export-control / launch disruption | $30M | GP1 → … → FN1 (Moderate) |
| **Programme (GA1)** | AURORA-GEO development overrun | −$12M FCF *(GEO)* | RA-02 → RC-02 → FN1 (Moderate) |
| **Financial (FN1)** | IPO window collapse / distressed raise | *dilution-priced* | **the sink itself** |

> **Why this matters to the board:** a flat register hides the convergence and invites you to fund risks one-by-one. RIM surfaces it — so capital goes to the **shared edges**, the mitigations that protect the convergence point and therefore buy down *several* families at once. That is the commonality view, and it is the entire reason to model risk as a graph.

## 3. The worked example: pricing the cyber tail
S1 is the hero scenario because it shows the whole mechanism in one event. Elicited as a business consequence (not a technical checklist) and quantified in the board's own currency:

| S1 — NOC compromise | EBIT (3-yr) | FY2028 FCF | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Best (P10) | −$40M | −$60M | absorbed within plan |
| **Realistic (P50)** | **−$69M** | **−$79M** | turns +$13M EBIT into ≈ −$41M |
| Pessimistic (P90) | −$110M | **−$118M** | **breaches the −$100M trigger → becomes FN1** |

In the pessimistic case, a *cyber* event stops being an IT problem and **becomes a financing event** — it crosses the trigger, forces the distressed raise, and lands on the IPO. That is the **S1 → FN1** edge, made literal.

## 4. The decision: what to spend, and what it buys
With the tail priced, the board can compare **money spent** against **loss removed** — the only risk question a board ever truly asks:

- **The comfortable answer fails.** The existing **$50M cyber-insurance tower** *does not pay* for S1: its **war / state-actor exclusion** is written against exactly this attacker profile, and attribution takes days. Paper coverage, not real coverage.
- **The controls that move the curve cost less than the tail.** Separating the shared Denver/Dublin identity plane (**~$4M**) and a clean control-plane recovery capability (**~$1.5M**) — **~$5.5M one-time** — pull the pessimistic case **back above the −$100M trigger.**

| | Figure |
|---|---|
| Control spend (one-time) | **~$5.5M** |
| Realistic feared loss removed | **~$69M** |
| Return on spend | **≈ 12×** |
| What it protects | the **~$250M IPO** — FCF held above the trigger |

The board doesn't buy "security." It buys a **quantified movement in the distribution**, with the avoided loss on the same slide — and **severs the path from a cyber event to a collapsed IPO.**

## 5. The second act: AURORA-GEO and the second P&L perimeter
Risk governance does not stop at the operating constellation. ODT is also running a **second, smaller bet** — the **AURORA-GEO** optical-relay demonstrator, ESA/NASA co-funded, **~$50M total** through its **PDR gate in Q1 2027**, of which ODT's *own* cash exposure is only **~40% (~$20M)**, burning **~$10M in FY2028**.

This is its own P&L perimeter (**BP-GEO**), and it shows the board something important: **risk converges across perimeters, not just within one.** A development overrun on GEO (family **GA1**) drains ODT's internal share into company cash — **RA-02 → RC-02** — and lands on the *same* FN1 sink as the cyber, supply-chain, and regulatory families. The second bet is governed against the same IPO objective, on the same graph, with the same method. It is the equity story's second act and its FCF-cushion tension in one perimeter.

## 6. What the board gets from RIM
- **A priced risk picture, not adjectives** — every family in EBIT and FCF, comparable on one axis.
- **Capital allocation by leverage** — spend on the shared edges that protect the convergence point; ~12× on the first control portfolio alone.
- **A defensible IPO disclosure** — a *modelled, bounded* residual the company owns and discloses, rather than an unmodelled tail no underwriter will honour.
- **A repeatable method** — the same elicitation → pricing → mitigation loop, pointed next at the other five families. Dramatised end-to-end in [[THE NUMBER - Season 1 (external)|THE NUMBER]] (quantification) and [[THE NUMBER - Season 2 (mitigation)|Season 2]] (return-on-spend).

> **The shift:** from *"are we secure?"* (unanswerable) to *"what do we spend, and what does it buy?"* (a board decision) — across every risk that converges on the listing.
